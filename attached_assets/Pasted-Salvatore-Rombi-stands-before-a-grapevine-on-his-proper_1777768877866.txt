Salvatore Rombi stands before a grapevine on his property and examines the cane branches protruding from the top of the twisted stalk.
“Nah, we don’t want that one,” he says, snipping off a thin branch. “But this one, this one is going to bud.”

He prunes four or five branches, leaving just two promising ones on opposite sides of each other, and ties them like long arms to the guide wires.
One down, 1,848 vines left to go.
It’s pruning season at Rombi’s Carmel Valley Vineyard, perched on a 1,650-foot chalk ridge in Cachagua that doubles as his backyard. With just 11/3 acres, Rombi believes he qualifies as the smallest licensed and bonded vineyard in the state.
He has 14 rows of Cabernet Sauvignon, seven rows of Merlot and two rows of still experimental Petit Syrah. And two rescue dogs, a terrier named Alibaba and Bella the German shorthaired pointer, who like to tear up and down the rows keeping things interesting.

And because his vineyard is so petite, and because it’s his baby, Rombi is a one-man winemaking operation.
He prunes, he picks, he crushes, he ages the wine in French oak barrels in his garage, and he bottles and labels himself. He tends the soil sustainably, using cover crops rather than chemicals to re-introduce nitrogen into the ground. Every day he is in his vineyard, tasting the grapes to calculate when they are at their peak for picking. And at the tasting room he opened in Carmel Valley Village in August 2013, Rombi is the sole employee, pouring just two types of Bordeaux-style reds from behind the counter.
Production is beyond limited. He produces fewer than 200 cases a year, and vintages run out quickly. Besides his website and tasting room, the only places that carry Rombi wines are three Carmel Valley restaurants: Will’s Fargo, Corkscrew Cafe and Bernardus Lodge; and La Mia Cucina in Pacific Grove, a former family restaurant now owned by friends. Bottles range in price from $65 to $135.

“This is for retirement; it’s not to expand,” says Rombi, a longtime Carmel Valley real estate agent who takes business calls on a cell phone while working in his vineyard. “It’s to make the best wine I possibly can.”
It’s been his dream since he was 13, working as the wine buyer (yes, you read that right) in his Monterey family’s Italian restaurant, Rappa’s, on Monterey’s historic Fisherman’s Wharf. He grew up in kitchens with his parents, his five brothers and sisters, aunts and uncles, and developed a passion for family cooking and a sixth sense for wine.
Rombi and his partner, local radio station executive Kathy Baker, planted their backyard south-facing vineyard with drought-resistant rootstock in 1998. They sought advice from Todd Kenyon, who helped design the vineyard for Bernardus Vineyards and Winery. Rombi read piles of books on winemaking, and then turned to the Monterey Peninsula guru of viticulture, Greg Vita of Big Sur. Vita spent a dozen years making wine at Spring Mountain Vineyards in St. Helena, as well as wines for Frog’s Leap, Dunn, Philip Togni and Chimney Rock. He’s now the chief winemaker at Galante Vineyards, which is just up the road from Rombi’s vineyard.
The Cachagua area (pronounced kuh-SHAW-wa) is favored by Carmel Valley vintners for its chalky, sandy soil that makes the vines struggle to produce fruit; for daytime temperatures that mimic Napa; and nightly coastal fog that reduces ripening time per day so the grapes hang about a month longer on the vine to create more rich and complex wines.

Jack Galante lets Rombi use his de-stemmer and crusher machine, and then Rombi stores his wine for two years in French oak barrels in his own garage.
“The winemaking community here is so helpful; they want everyone to succeed,” Rombi says.
Rombi unveiled his first Merlot and Cabernet Sauvignon in 2006. That year, at the Los Angeles International Wine and Spirits Competition, Rombi won a silver medal for his Merlot, and a silver in the packaging category for his minimalist black label, which was designed by leading wine label designers Jeffrey Caldewey and Chuck House of Icon Design.
He’s been encouraged to enter more competitions but says he has to be careful not to overexpose his product because he isn’t sure he could keep up if demand got too high for his boutique wines. His future retirement is supposed to stay fun, not morph into a demanding job.

Rombi and Baker’s favorite way to market their wines is to have guests stay in their vineyard cottage, overlooking their grapevines to the north, and the westward vineyards of Heller Estate and Chateau Julien. The cottage, which rents for $250 a night in the low season and $295 May through October, is a quiet big sky retreat, with knot-pine walls, a full kitchen stocked with Peet’s coffee, eggs and juice; a living room, a balcony with a barbecue, and a video and book library. The thread count of the sheets is a luxurious 1,600. Below the second-story cottage is Rombi’s barrel storage and homemade lab, where he measures pH levels and hand labels his bottles.
He proudly gives cottage guests a tour through his vineyard, and brings them to the garage for barrel tastings.
“This one is young and jumpy, like a teenager,” he says, sipping a 2013 Cabernet from the barrel. He puts the cork back, and then siphons a taste from the neighboring barrel, harvested on the same day. The second taste is smooth and almost ready to bottle.
“See that? Same grapes, same harvest, put in barrels from the same manufacturer on the same day,” he says. “It’s amazing. I think it’s the wood. One barrel was made with wood from one tree and the other barrel another tree.”

Learning about wine is a perpetual adventure for Rombi. He compares it to making a pasta sauce. A recipe is just the starting place. The X factor is in growing your own ingredients, the handed-down family recipes, the years spent learning to taste and seat-of-the-pants flavor correcting. When he pours his wine, it’s an expression of his love for the place he grew up, his family and the new friends he’s about to make.
“I love planting things and making delicious things that are expressions of place. Having your own winery is just a far-out thing to do.”
